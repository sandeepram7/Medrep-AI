## Pain / Fever / Anti‑inflammatory

**Paracetamol (100 words)**
Paracetamol primarily inhibits COX-3, a central nervous system variant of cyclooxygenase enzymes, and weakly inhibits COX-2. This selective central action reduces prostaglandin synthesis in the brain, providing effective analgesia and antipyresis. Unlike NSAIDs, it exhibits minimal peripheral anti-inflammatory effects due to limited COX inhibition at inflammation sites. Its safety profile allows use across all age groups, including pregnancy, with primary metabolism via glucuronidation and sulfation. Therapeutic effects peak at 30–60 minutes, lasting 4–6 hours, making it ideal for fever control and mild–moderate pain management in outpatient settings.

**Ibuprofen (100 words)**
Ibuprofen is a non-selective COX-1/COX-2 inhibitor that blocks arachidonic acid conversion to prostaglandins at peripheral injury sites. This produces robust anti-inflammatory, analgesic, and antipyretic effects through multiple mechanisms: direct COX inhibition, reduced leukotriene production via LOX pathway shunting prevention, and platelet aggregation inhibition. Its peripheral predominance distinguishes it from paracetamol’s central action. Peak effects occur within 1–2 hours with 6–8 hour duration. GI mucosal protection is better than aspirin but requires food co-administration to minimize ulceration risk. The R-enantiomer converts to the active S-form in vivo.

**Diclofenac (100 words)**
Diclofenac demonstrates preferential COX-2 inhibition alongside moderate COX-1 blockade, delivering superior peripheral anti-inflammatory potency compared to ibuprofen. Additional mechanisms include voltage-gated sodium channel inhibition and peroxisome proliferator-activated receptor gamma (PPAR-γ) modulation. It exhibits high potency among arylacetic acid NSAIDs for prostaglandin suppression at inflammation sites. Topical formulations achieve high local concentrations with minimal systemic exposure. Hepatic metabolism produces active metabolites with 1.2–2 hour half-life. The dual inhibition profile explains superior efficacy in acute musculoskeletal injuries and post-surgical inflammation despite an increased cardiovascular thrombotic risk profile.

**Aceclofenac + Paracetamol (100 words)**
Aceclofenac, a phenylacetic acid derivative, preferentially inhibits COX-2 (IC50 ratio COX-1:COX-2 = 30:1) while stimulating glycosaminoglycan synthesis in chondrocytes, providing cartilage protection. Paracetamol complements this with central COX-3 inhibition for analgesia without peripheral inflammation. This fixed-dose combination leverages complementary mechanisms: aceclofenac’s potent anti-inflammatory action addresses tissue damage while paracetamol provides rapid brain-mediated pain relief. Synergistic effects exceed individual components in acute pain models. Aceclofenac’s active metabolites prolong duration while paracetamol’s rapid onset ensures immediate relief, ideal for moderate–severe acute pain requiring both inflammatory control and analgesia.

**Aspirin 75/150 mg cardiac (100 words)**
Low-dose aspirin (75–150 mg) irreversibly acetylates the COX-1 serine residue 529 in platelets, selectively inhibiting thromboxane A2 (TXA2) synthesis while sparing endothelial prostacyclin (PGI2) production due to differential enzyme recovery rates. Platelets lack nuclei, preventing COX-1 resynthesis during their 7–10 day lifespan, ensuring sustained antiplatelet effect. Vascular endothelium regenerates COX-2 rapidly, maintaining PGI2-mediated vasodilation. This creates a favorable TXA2:PGI2 ratio shift, preventing platelet aggregation without vascular compromise. Enteric coating minimizes GI bleeding risk. Effect persists 7–10 days post-dose, enabling alternate-day dosing in some stable coronary disease regimens.

## Antibiotics

**Amoxicillin (100 words)**
Amoxicillin, an aminopenicillin, binds penicillin-binding proteins (PBPs 1A, 1B, 2, 3), inhibiting transpeptidation during bacterial peptidoglycan cross-linking essential for cell wall integrity. It is active against Gram-positive aerobes (Streptococcus, Enterococcus) and some Gram-negatives. Time-dependent killing requires T>MIC optimization. The β-lactam ring acylates active serine PBPs, preventing cell wall synthesis while activating autolysins causing lysis. Poor β-lactamase stability limits Gram-negative coverage. Rapid oral absorption (70–90%) achieves high serum levels. It is primarily renally excreted and remains usable in hepatic impairment. A 1–1.5 hour half-life necessitates TID/QID dosing for optimal efficacy.

**Amoxicillin + Clavulanic Acid (100 words)**
Amoxicillin inhibits cell wall synthesis via PBP binding while clavulanic acid, a β-lactamase suicide inhibitor, irreversibly acylates serine β-lactamase active sites, preserving amoxicillin activity against β-lactamase producers (S. aureus, H. influenzae, M. catarrhalis, some Enterobacteriaceae). A typical 4:1 ratio optimizes efficacy:safety. Clavulanate extends spectrum to β-lactamase–positive pathogens while maintaining time-dependent killing. Competitive antagonism overcomes some inducible β-lactamases. Diarrhea risk increases due to gut flora disruption. Twice-daily dosing improves adherence. Renal dose adjustment is required. It remains a common first-line choice for community respiratory and some GI infections despite resistance emergence.

**Azithromycin (100 words)**
Azithromycin, a 15-membered macrolide, reversibly binds the 50S ribosomal subunit 23S rRNA domain V, inhibiting translocation and early peptide chain elongation. It is bacteriostatic against most pathogens but bactericidal against select streptococci and H. influenzae. Exceptional tissue penetration (lung > plasma) and a long intracellular half-life (~68 hours) enable 3–5 day short courses. It is acid-stable with ~37% bioavailability. Accumulation in macrophages enhances activity against Legionella and Chlamydia. Minimal CYP450 interaction distinguishes it from older macrolides. It covers atypical pneumonia, chlamydia, and streptococcal pharyngitis. QT prolongation risk requires caution with other QT-prolonging drugs.

**Cefixime (100 words)**
Cefixime is a third-generation cephalosporin with enhanced Gram-negative activity via PBP3 affinity, particularly Enterobacteriaceae. Poor PBP2 binding limits anti-pseudomonal activity. Its β-lactam ring inhibits transpeptidation during cell wall synthesis, producing time-dependent bactericidal effects; efficacy correlates with T>MIC >40–50%. The oral prodrug achieves 40–50% bioavailability. It penetrates respiratory and urinary tissues well and has some CSF activity. It is stable against TEM-1 β-lactamases but vulnerable to ESBLs. Twice-daily dosing is convenient for outpatient therapy. It covers gonorrhea, urinary infections, and some respiratory infections, with limited anaerobic activity, and requires renal dose adjustment.

**Ceftriaxone (100 words)**
Ceftriaxone is a third-generation cephalosporin with dual PBP2/PBP3 binding, conferring broad-spectrum activity against many Gram-negative and Gram-positive organisms. High PBP affinity ensures bactericidal cell wall inhibition. Exceptional CSF penetration (15–25% of serum levels) supports meningitis treatment. A long 6–9 hour half-life allows once-daily dosing in many indications. Biliary elimination provides partial hepatic clearance. Time-dependent killing is optimized by extended or continuous infusions. It is stable against many β-lactamases, though ESBL-mediated resistance is emerging. It covers meningitis, gonorrhea, and intra-abdominal infections. Displacement of bilirubin from albumin in neonates requires caution.

**Cefuroxime (100 words)**
Cefuroxime is a second-generation cephalosporin with balanced Gram-positive and Gram-negative coverage via PBP1/3 affinity. Improved β-lactamase stability versus first-generation agents enhances activity against H. influenzae and Moraxella. It inhibits cell wall synthesis in a time-dependent manner. The oral axetil prodrug achieves ~40% bioavailability. It penetrates sinus and middle-ear tissues well, making it useful in sinusitis and otitis media. It covers community respiratory pathogens and some UTIs, but is less potent against Enterobacteriaceae than third-generation cephalosporins. Anaerobic activity is limited. It is renally excreted and requires dose adjustment in renal impairment.

**Metronidazole (100 words)**
Metronidazole is reduced by nitroimidazole reductase in anaerobic bacteria and protozoa, converting its 5-nitro group to cytotoxic intermediates that damage DNA, proteins, and membranes. Selective anaerobic toxicity spares aerobes lacking these reductases. It is bactericidal against Bacteroides, Clostridia, and protozoa such as Giardia, Trichomonas, and Entamoeba. It penetrates abscesses and CSF well. A disulfiram-like reaction occurs with alcohol. Neurotoxicity risk increases with prolonged high doses. It is hepatically metabolized with renal excretion of metabolites. It covers intra-abdominal infections, C. difficile, trichomoniasis, and bacterial vaginosis, and is generally considered safe in pregnancy (category B).

**Doxycycline (100 words)**
Doxycycline, a second-generation tetracycline, binds the 30S ribosomal subunit at the A-site, preventing aminoacyl-tRNA binding and protein synthesis. It has broad-spectrum bacteriostatic activity, including atypicals such as Rickettsia and Chlamydia. Tissue penetration is excellent (prostate, lung, skin). A long 16–22 hour half-life enables once- or twice-daily dosing. Adverse effects include photosensitivity, GI intolerance, and risk of esophageal ulceration if taken without water or before lying down. Chelation with divalent cations necessitates separation from antacids and dairy. It covers acne, malaria prophylaxis, Lyme disease, and respiratory infections. Avoid in children <8 years due to tooth staining.

**Levofloxacin (100 words)**
Levofloxacin, a fluoroquinolone, inhibits bacterial DNA gyrase and topoisomerase IV, preventing DNA supercoiling necessary for replication and transcription. It exhibits concentration-dependent bactericidal activity; efficacy correlates with AUC:MIC >30–40. It has broad-spectrum Gram-positive, Gram-negative, and atypical coverage, with some Pseudomonas activity. Tissue and biofilm penetration are excellent. It is minimally metabolized by CYP enzymes and primarily renally excreted. Risks include QT prolongation, tendonitis and tendon rupture, dysglycemia, and C. difficile infection. Magnesium and other cations reduce absorption. It covers community-acquired pneumonia, complicated UTI/pyelonephritis, and chronic bacterial prostatitis, with renal dose adjustment needed.

**Ciprofloxacin (100 words)**
Ciprofloxacin, an early fluoroquinolone, inhibits DNA gyrase in Gram-negatives and topoisomerase IV in Gram-positives, causing double-strand DNA breaks and bactericidal effects. It shows concentration-dependent killing with AUC:MIC-driven efficacy. It has enhanced Gram-negative and Pseudomonas activity compared with some newer agents but relatively poor S. pneumoniae coverage. It penetrates urinary tract and prostate tissues well, making it useful in complicated UTI, acute pyelonephritis, and chronic bacterial prostatitis. It is largely CYP-independent but shares class risks: QT prolongation, tendon rupture, CNS effects, and C. difficile infection. Chelation with cations reduces absorption; renal adjustment is required.

## Diabetes

**Metformin (100 words)**
Metformin, a biguanide, activates AMP-activated protein kinase (AMPK), inhibiting hepatic gluconeogenesis (via PEPCK and G6Pase suppression), increasing peripheral insulin sensitivity through GLUT4 translocation, and decreasing intestinal glucose absorption. It is weight-neutral and does not cause hypoglycemia when used alone. Lactic acidosis is rare (around 0.03 cases per 1000 patient-years) but risk increases with significant renal impairment. GI intolerance limits adherence in some patients; extended-release formulations improve tolerability. It is first-line T2DM monotherapy in major guidelines and has shown cardiovascular mortality reduction in UKPDS 34. It is renally excreted and contraindicated when eGFR <30 ml/min.

**Glimepiride (100 words)**
Glimepiride, a second-generation sulfonylurea, binds the SUR1 subunit of pancreatic β-cell KATP channels, closing the channels, depolarizing the membrane, opening voltage-gated Ca2+ channels, and triggering insulin exocytosis. It lowers both fasting and postprandial glucose. Hypoglycemia risk is significant, especially in the elderly and those with renal impairment. Weight gain of 1–3 kg is common. It is hepatically metabolized via CYP2C9, leading to interactions with inhibitors or inducers. A 5–9 hour terminal half-life supports once-daily dosing. It has been associated with microvascular complication benefit in older trials but lacks the CV outcome benefits seen with newer classes.

**Human Insulin (100 words)**
Human insulin binds the insulin receptor tyrosine kinase, activating the PI3K/Akt pathway and promoting GLUT4 translocation, increasing glucose uptake into muscle and adipose tissue while enhancing glycogen, fat, and protein synthesis. It suppresses lipolysis, proteolysis, and hepatic glucose output. Different formulations—rapid-acting (lispro/aspart/glulisine), short-acting (regular), intermediate (NPH), and long-acting (glargine/detemir/degludec)—optimize basal–bolus coverage. Hypoglycemia is the main risk, requiring regular glucose monitoring and dose individualization. Weight gain is expected with improved glycemic control. Antibody formation is rare with modern human and analog preparations.

## Cardiovascular

**Amlodipine (100 words)**
Amlodipine, a dihydropyridine calcium channel blocker, selectively inhibits L-type voltage-gated Ca2+ channels in vascular smooth muscle, preventing calcium entry, inducing arteriolar vasodilation, and reducing peripheral resistance. At therapeutic doses it has minimal effect on cardiac conduction and contractility. A long 30–50 hour half-life allows once-daily dosing with smooth blood pressure control. It is metabolized by CYP3A4. It is effective for hypertension and stable angina through coronary and peripheral vasodilation. Ankle edema is a dose-related common adverse effect. It is preferred in isolated systolic hypertension, especially in older patients.

**Losartan (100 words)**
Losartan, the first angiotensin receptor blocker (ARB), selectively blocks AT1 receptors, inhibiting angiotensin II–mediated vasoconstriction, aldosterone secretion, and sympathetic activation, while allowing AT2 receptor–mediated vasodilatory effects. It also has a modest uric acid–lowering effect compared with ACE inhibitors. It is hepatically metabolized via CYP2C9/3A4 to an active metabolite (E-3174) that is ~6 times more potent and has a longer half-life. It is indicated for hypertension, diabetic nephropathy, and heart failure. Risks include hyperkalemia and acute kidney injury, especially in bilateral renal artery stenosis or severe volume depletion. It is contraindicated in pregnancy.

**Telmisartan (100 words)**
Telmisartan is an ARB with very high AT1 receptor affinity and partial PPAR-γ agonist activity, which may confer favorable metabolic effects. It has the longest half-life among ARBs (~24 hours), enabling true 24-hour blood pressure control with once-daily dosing. It undergoes minimal CYP metabolism and is mainly excreted via the biliary route, reducing drug–drug interaction potential. Its hydrophobic structure enhances tissue penetration. It is indicated for hypertension and reduction of cardiovascular risk in high-risk patients. It shares ARB class risks (hyperkalemia, AKI, pregnancy contraindication) and may offer more sustained BP lowering versus losartan.

**Atorvastatin (100 words)**
Atorvastatin, a synthetic statin, competitively inhibits HMG-CoA reductase, reducing hepatic cholesterol synthesis and upregulating LDL receptors, thereby increasing LDL clearance. It has pleiotropic effects including plaque stabilization, anti-inflammatory actions, and improved endothelial function. It is extensively metabolized by CYP3A4 to active metabolites. At commonly used doses it achieves ~40–60% LDL reduction. Trials such as CARDS have shown primary prevention benefit in high-risk patients. Myopathy and rhabdomyolysis are rare but increased with fibrates or strong CYP3A4 inhibitors. Liver enzyme monitoring is advised, particularly at higher doses.

**Rosuvastatin (100 words)**
Rosuvastatin is a hepatoselective statin with minimal extrahepatic uptake, resulting in potent LDL cholesterol reduction—up to ~60% at higher doses. It undergoes limited CYP2C9-mediated metabolism, with reno-biliary excretion. Exposure is higher in some Asian populations, warranting lower starting doses. Trials such as JUPITER have demonstrated primary and secondary prevention benefits. It carries a small dose-related risk of new-onset diabetes and class-typical myopathy and rhabdomyolysis risks. Routine liver function monitoring is recommended at initiation and dose escalation.

**Clopidogrel (100 words)**
Clopidogrel is a prodrug metabolized by CYP2C19 (and other CYPs) to an active thiol metabolite that irreversibly binds the platelet P2Y12 ADP receptor, preventing GPIIb/IIIa activation, fibrinogen binding, and aggregation. It achieves ~20–30% inhibition of ADP-induced platelet aggregation. CYP2C19 poor metabolizers exhibit reduced antiplatelet response, though routine genetic testing remains controversial. Typical regimens use a 300–600 mg loading dose followed by 75 mg daily. It is indicated for secondary prevention after MI, stroke, and in peripheral arterial disease, and in dual antiplatelet therapy after PCI. Bleeding is the main adverse event.

## Gastric / Acid Reflux / Antiemetic

**Pantoprazole (100 words)**
Pantoprazole is a proton pump inhibitor (PPI) that irreversibly binds cysteine residues on the gastric H+/K+ ATPase in parietal cells, blocking the final step of acid secretion and achieving 80–95% acid suppression with repeated dosing. It has relatively low dependence on CYP2C19 compared with omeprazole, reducing some drug–drug interaction risk. IV and oral formulations allow flexible inpatient and outpatient use. It is indicated for erosive esophagitis, GERD, Zollinger–Ellison syndrome, and as part of H. pylori eradication regimens. Long-term use is associated with risks such as osteoporosis-related fractures and C. difficile infection.

**Omeprazole (100 words)**
Omeprazole, the first PPI, irreversibly inhibits the gastric H+/K+ ATPase proton pump after conversion to an active sulfenamide intermediate in the acidic canaliculi. It is extensively metabolized by CYP2C19 and CYP3A4, resulting in significant interindividual variation and drug interaction potential. Indications include GERD, peptic ulcer disease, and H. pylori eradication regimens. Prolonged acid suppression can cause hypomagnesemia, B12 malabsorption, and increased risk of enteric infections. Inhibitory effects on CYP2C19 can reduce activation of clopidogrel, warranting caution or use of alternative PPIs in cardiac patients on dual antiplatelet therapy.

**Rabeprazole (100 words)**
Rabeprazole is a PPI that both covalently binds the H+/K+ ATPase proton pump and undergoes a non-enzymatic sulfenamide pathway, leading to rapid and potent acid suppression. It has the least dependence on CYP2C19 among PPIs, resulting in more consistent exposure across genotypes and fewer interactions with CYP2C19 substrates. It is indicated for healing and maintenance of erosive esophagitis, GERD, and as part of H. pylori eradication regimens. Onset is relatively rapid compared with omeprazole. Long-term risks overlap with the PPI class, including fractures and enteric infection risk.

**Ondansetron (100 words)**
Ondansetron is a selective 5-HT3 receptor antagonist that blocks serotonin-mediated activation of vagal afferent nerves in the gut and chemoreceptor trigger zone in the area postrema, reducing nausea and vomiting from chemotherapy, radiotherapy, and surgery. It is metabolized mainly by CYP3A4 and CYP2D6. It is available in oral, IV, and orally disintegrating tablet (ODT) formulations. A key safety concern is QT interval prolongation and risk of arrhythmias, especially at higher IV doses or in patients with underlying cardiac disease or electrolyte disturbances. It is generally well tolerated; common side effects include headache and constipation.

## Respiratory / Allergy

**Salbutamol (100 words)**
Salbutamol is a short-acting β2-selective adrenergic agonist that binds β2 receptors in bronchial smooth muscle, activating adenylate cyclase, increasing cAMP, and phosphorylating myosin light chain kinase (MLCK), resulting in bronchodilation. It has rapid onset (within ~5 minutes) and 4–6 hour duration. It is used for acute relief of asthma and COPD exacerbations, and prevention of exercise-induced bronchospasm. Regular overuse can lead to tachyphylaxis and indicates poor underlying control. Common side effects include tremor, tachycardia, palpitations, and hypokalemia at high doses.

**Montelukast + Levocetirizine (100 words)**
This fixed-dose combination pairs montelukast, a selective cysteinyl leukotriene receptor 1 (CysLT1) antagonist, with levocetirizine, a non-sedating H1 antihistamine. Montelukast blocks LTD4-mediated bronchoconstriction, mucus secretion, edema, and eosinophil recruitment, providing anti-inflammatory control in asthma and allergic rhinitis. Levocetirizine, the R-enantiomer of cetirizine, acts as an H1 inverse agonist with minimal blood–brain barrier penetration, reducing sneezing, itching, and rhinorrhea with limited sedation. Once-daily oral dosing improves adherence for patients with coexisting asthma and allergic rhinitis. Montelukast carries neuropsychiatric warnings (mood changes, suicidality), while levocetirizine is primarily renally excreted and usually well tolerated.

**Budesonide (100 words)**
Budesonide is an inhaled glucocorticoid that binds cytoplasmic glucocorticoid receptors, translocating to the nucleus to inhibit transcription factors such as NF-κB and AP-1, thereby suppressing proinflammatory cytokines, chemokines, and adhesion molecules. It reduces airway eosinophilia, decreases hyperresponsiveness, and improves lung function. Inhaled delivery maximizes local effect while minimizing systemic exposure. It is used for asthma maintenance and to reduce COPD exacerbations in selected patients. Rinsing the mouth and using a spacer help prevent oral candidiasis and dysphonia. High doses or prolonged use can still contribute to systemic steroid effects.
